The big data revolution and how it affects economics

What is big data?

Big data is the collection of immense data sets that are varied and complex in structure. The data collected is generated by: search queries, online transactions, health records, stock market trading, business documents, social networking and taxes. There are many more sources for data but these are just some large categories to show you where we get the information from. With the increase in data, it has become difficult to visualize and analyze this data. This is where informatics professionals help to make the lives of non-technical people easier.

How does Big Data relate to Economics?

To understand how these two topics relate, you must first understand what economics is. If you have ever wondered why some countries are rich and some are poor or what causes recessions, this is why we have economics. Economics is a social science that involves the production, distribution and consumption of goods and services. It focuses on how individuals, businesses and governments make choices with the allocation of resources to satisfy wants and needs. The rise of big data allows for a new opportunity for economic predictions.

Visualization of Big Data

Data visualization dates back to the 17th century where humans started using maps, graphs and pie charts. One of the most famous first uses of visualization was Charles Minard’s mapping of Napoleon’s invasion of Russia. Visualization was only in it’s infancy then, the computer revolution truly lit the fire under data visualization.


Visualization is very important when trying to construct a representation of one’s research. Economist’s are diving deep into how they can make compelling arguments with presenting a visual representation of their research.

Types of Economic Systems

Market

Market economies are based on consumers their bying decisions instead of being under government control. The laws of supply and demand direct the production of goods and services. Businesses sell their products are the highest price the consumer will pay. This kind of system also allows consumers to look for the lowest prices on goods and services they want.

Command

In a command economy, the government has a monopoly on all economic activity. An example of this is communism. Command economies are less malleable than market economies and react slower to changes in consumer purchasing patterns and fluctuations in supply and demand.

Mixed

A mixed economy combines qualities of market and command systems into one. A mixed economy has three of the following characteristics of a market economy. First, it protects private property. Second, it allows the free market and the laws of supply and demand to determine prices. Third, it is driven by the motivation of the self-interest of individuals.
